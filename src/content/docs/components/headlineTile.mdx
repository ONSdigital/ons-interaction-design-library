The Headline tile is a simple but highly visible component used to highlight a single standout statistic. It forms the building block of the [Headline facts and figures](/components/headlinefactsfigures) component and is not intended to be used across a page.

Its purpose is to surface key data quickly and clearly. The design relies on strong hierarchy, generous spacing, and restraint in content to maintain impact. Where appropriate, geographical information can be displayed to help users understand the scope of the statistic at a glance.

Although visually simple, careful content guidance is essential to preserve balance and readability.

## Purpose and goals

The primary purpose of the Headline tile is to present a single key statistic in a way that is immediately visible and understandable. It allows users to quickly grasp important information without needing to read through detailed content. By presenting information in a clear, concise format, it supports quick decision-making and helps users find the insights they need efficiently.

The Headline tile is designed to:
* Present one key statistic clearly and prominently
* Support quick scanning behaviour
* Reinforce important messages within a page
* Provide context through concise supporting text
It should feel confident and uncluttered. The number or key figure is the focal point, supported — not overshadowed — by surrounding text.

## When to use this component

This component should only be used within the Headline Facts and Figures component, where it is combined with other tiles to create a cohesive presentation of key statistics. It is not intended to be used in isolation across a page.

Use a Headline tile when:
* There is a single statistic that stands on its own
* A key figure summarises part of a larger story
* You need to support scanning behaviour
It should not be used for detailed explanation, complex data, or multiple competing statistics. Each tile should communicate one clear idea.

## Structure and anatomy

The Headline tile consists of four core elements:

1. Figure title - a short label describing what the statistic represents
2. Geographical information - identifies the geographical area that the statistic relates to, where applicable
3. Key figure - the primary data point and the visual focal point of the component
4. Supporting information - provides additional context, such as the time period or other essential details

The visual hierarchy should always prioritise the key figure. The title and supporting information provide clarity but should not compete visually with the main number.

## Content guidance and visual balance

Because publishers can input their own content, strong guidance is necessary to maintain consistency and balance.

### Figure title

Titles should be as short as possible. Ideally, they should be a brief phrase rather than a sentence. Long titles increase vertical height and reduce visual impact.

Avoid:
* Multi-line headings where possible
* Explanatory sentences

Prefer:
* Clear, direct labels
* Plain language

### Geographical information

Geographical information identifies the area covered by the statistic, helping users understand its scope without needing to refer to the supporting information.

Where included, it should:
* Clearly identify the relevant geography (for example, UK, England or Wales)
* Be concise and easy to scan
* Use recognised geographical names
* Avoid repeating information already included elsewhere in the tile

This field should only describe geographical coverage. Additional context, such as dates or methodology, should be included within the supporting information where appropriate.

### Key figure

The key figure should be concise and, wherever possible, fit on a single line. Long phrases or explanatory text within the key figure reduce impact and may break the layout.

Avoid:
* Full sentences as the main statistic
* Excessively long qualifiers within the number

Prefer:
* A clean number or percentage
* Short qualifiers only when essential (for example, a unit)

If additional explanation is required, it should sit within the supporting text.

### Supporting information

Supporting text should provide brief clarification or context that is not already conveyed by the title, geographical information or key figure.

Keep this text:
* Descriptive but short
* Limited to one or two short lines
* Free of complex methodological detail

If more detail is required, it should appear in the main body content rather than within the tile.

Restraint is key. Overloading the tile with text undermines its at-a-glance purpose.

The figure title is visually and structurally separated from the key figure and supporting information. This separation helps maintain balance when multiple tiles are displayed alongside one another.

By anchoring the title at the top of the tile and the supporting information at the bottom, the key figure remains visually centred and prominent. If a title or supporting text wraps onto an additional line, the tile can expand naturally without disrupting the overall alignment of neighbouring tiles.

This approach ensures consistent visual rhythm and balance across a row, even when content lengths vary.

## Responsive behaviour

The component must adapt gracefully across screen sizes.

On wider viewports:
* The key figure remains large and visually dominant
* Spacing supports clear separation between title, figure, and supporting text

On narrower viewports:
* The key figure text size should reduce proportionally to prevent wrapping or overflow
* Layout should prioritise readability over scale
* Content may wrap naturally, but the hierarchy must remain clear

Designers should test realistic content at smaller breakpoints to ensure that long numbers, percentages, or units do not break the layout unexpectedly.

The aim is to preserve impact while maintaining legibility and comfort on touch devices.

## Behaviour and usage guidance

Headline tiles may optionally link to related content, such as a statistical article or dataset. If used as links, the entire tile should feel interactive and consistent with other interactive components on the page.

Where geographical information is displayed, it should be used consistently across all Headline tiles within the same Headline Facts and Figures component.

When grouped within the Headline facts and figures component, visual weight between tiles should feel balanced. Avoid one tile appearing disproportionately dominant due to excessive text or unusually long content.

Consistency across tiles is important. Similar tone, length, and formatting should be maintained wherever possible.

## Linking behaviour

In some contexts, the figure title can act as a link to more detailed content, such as a statistical article or dataset that explains the headline figure in full.

When used as a link:
* The title should use the standard link style for the page context.
* Only the title should be interactive, not the entire tile.
* The link destination should provide meaningful depth or context to justify navigation.

In other contexts — such as when the headline figures sit within the content they relate to — the title should remain plain text.

This flexible approach allows the component to support both navigational and informational use cases, while maintaining a consistent visual structure.

## Design considerations in Figma

In Figma, the Headline tile should remain a structured component with defined text styles and spacing tokens applied consistently.

Designers should:
* Test with realistic but controlled content
* Avoid overriding text styles to accommodate overly long copy
* Maintain spacing in line with the layout grid

If content does not fit comfortably within the component, this is a signal that the copy should be refined rather than the component adjusted.

The effectiveness of the Headline tile lies in clarity, hierarchy, and disciplined content.
